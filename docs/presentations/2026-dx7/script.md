In 2016, Bruno Mars released an album called _24k Magic_. This was coming off the massive success of “Uptown Funk,” which had major retro vibes. 24k Magic was also deliberately retro in sound, according to Mars. In an interview with Rolling Stone, he says the album is meant to sound like early 90s R&B—like slow-dancing at a junior high dance to “Before I Let You Go” by Blackstreet.

This comes through clearly in "Versace on the Floor", which was the third single off the album. Let's listen.

Music critics in 2016 all make note of “Versace”’s throwback sound. They compare it to:

- Marvin Gaye's "sexual healing"
- 1984 New Edition
- Freddie Jackson's "Rock Me Tonight"
- an old Shanice song that wouldn't have sounded out of place on the original 90210 soundtrack, and
- Boyz II Men's "I'll Make Love to You" repurposed for the Dirty Dancing soundtrack.

Okay, so first, notice that all the songs are from a time period that I like to call the “long 1980s". Mainstream pop music and R&B has a relatively consistent sound through the early 1990s, especially when it comes to ballads. And one key component of these ballads, including all the ones in this list, is the use of electric piano.

Let's zoom in on the more specific song references. Take a listen to these excerpts of all 3 songs and see if you hear what I hear.

You could probably guess I wanted you to notice the pretty prominent electric piano in each track. Now if you listen super duper closely, they each actually use a different electric piano. But realistically, the subtle differences between these sounds kind of melt away when you listen to these songs all in sequence. For a normal listener, this all just sounds like one instrument: an '80s (or '90s) electric piano. My guess would be that, consciously or not, the reviewers making these comparisons all likewise heard their songs as sharing the same electric piano sound as Mars's single.

But for a scholar of an immensely popular 1980s synthesizer with a very famous electric piano preset, to me, the intro of "Versace" doesn’t sound like just any electric piano: the timbre is specifically that of a Yamaha DX7 electric piano preset. When I first heard this song, I immediately wanted to know whether Mars did in fact use a DX7 on the recording. But as I will show, definitive evidence one way or the other was hard to find.

[click through outline] In this talk, I'll use “Versace on the Floor” and the Yamaha DX7 E. PIANO 1 sound to illustrate the problems one might encounter when researching synthesizers in recent popular music. I argue that researchers don’t need to think of accurate instrument identification as a prerequisite for analyzing a song’s use of that instrument, because it’s already significant that the timbre suggests the instrument. I support this through my case study of the “Imagined DX7”: the DX7 as it lives in popular consciousness, derived from memories and imaginings of its most well-known factory presets. I explore how the E. PIANO 1 preset outgrew the DX7, complicating its sonic identity, but simultaneously solidifying a certain timbral signature that captures its essence—the Imagined DX7. I also discuss the technological changes—MIDI and the decline of the music trade magazine—that take away sources of truth for identifying instruments, a process that begins in the 1980s and continues to the present day. By considering timbre as the instrument, I show how researchers can analyze synthesizers in recent pop music through intertextuality and listener reception, areas that are unaffected or even enriched by possible inaccuracies in the instrument identification.

## DX7 History

I'll make the DX7 history part of this talk super speedy, because I have written and presented on it elsewhere. What you need to know today is that the DX7 has these preset sounds that shipped with it from the factory, and these presets were truly ubiquitous in music of the long 1980s. We're focusing on one preset, E. PIANO 1. Even if you don’t know it by name, if you listened to pop music in the 80s or 90s, you absolutely know the sound: [play]

The preset, often referred to as the "Rhodes sound," is the most widely used preset of the DX7—or some might say, over-used.

As the DX7’s E. PIANO 1 grew thoroughly entrenched in popular music, some musicians tweaked and customized this sound by layering it together with effects or other similar sounds. Synthesizer manufacturers looking to dethrone Yamaha from its market dominance—particularly Roland and Korg—likewise offered up their own takes on the electric piano patch that musicians had come to expect on their instruments. Today, musicians often use virtual instruments that mimic the signature DX7 sound. Through this process, over the course of more than forty years since its debut, the E. PIANO 1 sound evolves into something larger than just a preset on the DX7.

## Imitators

In the next section I examine electric piano factory presets on the two most significant post-DX7 synthesizers: the Roland D-50 and the Korg M1. Besides these synthesizers, I’ll also Dexed, a software-based DX7 substitute. By doing this, I want to show how the different sounds codify E. PIANO 1's signature timbral essence, which reifies the standing of E. PIANO 1 (and by extension the DX7) as an essential pop music sound, while simultaneously broadening the range of timbres a listener might reasonably conflate with that of E. PIANO 1.

### D-50

First, the D50. When Roland released the D-50 in 1987, four years after the DX7, it included a preset titled not “Electric Piano”, but “Synthetic Electric” (#28). The name feels significant to me because it's presenting the sound not as a Rhodes imitation, but a synthesizer imitation, presumably the DX7. 1987 was the height of the DX7’s dominance, and writers for trade magazines inevitably compared the two synths.

The two sounds are not identical by any means, but the DX7 influence is audible, and less savvy listeners may be forgiven for conflating them.

### M1

The Korg M1 was released in 1988 and eventually became the best-selling synthesizer of all time, overthrowing the DX7. Its four electric piano presets—E. Piano 1, 2, 3, and 4—all seem to imitate the DX7’s E. PIANO 1. Take a listen.

### Dexed

If accurately identifying a DX7 was complicated in the 1990s, it’s even more so today, since anyone with an internet connection can install virtual studio technologies (VSTs) that mimic the DX7. Dexed by Digital Suburban is a free and open-source VST that the developer says is “closely modeled on the original DX7 characteristics.” Listen to the side-by-side, and you'll see that the present-day music maker doesn’t need a DX7 to get an authentic DX7 sound—this free VST creates an indistinguishable imitation.

This is what I call the “Imagined DX7”: this collection of timbres, perceived as a unified instrument despite the variety of hardware and software used to produce it.

## The Imagined DX7

The Imagined DX7 is the result of the entrenchment of the lumped-together sounds of E. PIANO 1 and its imitators. As awareness of the DX7 sound grew, listeners began crediting the synth for sounds it didn't actually produce. The authentic DX7 sound also becomes blurred as these other sounds repeatedly pass as DX7 sounds.

Consider this quote from a podcast episode titled "The ’80s: Were They Really That Bad?" Here Bob Boilen says, or at least implies, that the DX7 is the common thread that ties together a bunch of songs they just played. The songs were "Let's Hear It for the Boy", "Wild Wild West," and "You Make My Dreams Come True”—and actually none of those songs use the DX7.

I want to emphasize that my point is not to trash-talk the guy who created Tiny Desk Concerts. More interesting to me than pointing out the mistake is what the mistake signifies: that many people imagine a sound comes from a DX7 even when it doesn’t.

Going in the opposite direction, the DX7 sound so saturated popular music of the long 1980s that the original sound of an actual Fender Rhodes was forgotten by a substantial population. A _Keyboard_ article about creating a good Rhodes sound suggests aiming for the sound on Michael Bolton’s “How Am I Supposed to Live Without You” (Jimmerson 1990), which…

I am pretty sure that is a DX7, not a Rhodes! Another article from _Keyboard_ reflects this when they write hyperbolically of “an entire generation of players out there who think a Rhodes really sounded like any of the myriad Yamaha DX7 sounds bearing its name”. Paul Wiffen goes even further, giving an anonymized anecdote about a “famous producer” criticizing Wiffen’s “painstakingly sampled” Rhodes for sounding “nothing like any electric piano he’d ever heard” and insinuates that the producer’s memory had been tainted by repeated exposure to DX7-esque electric pianos. Perhaps this is better understood as the Imagined Rhodes than the Imagined DX7, but either way, these incidents attest to the fuzzy boundaries of the DX7’s electric piano sound—many experienced listeners can’t discern where the DX7 begins and ends.

## Implications

The Imagined DX7 functions in part due to Cornelia Fales's (2002) “paradox of timbre.” When assessing the likely source of a timbre, we are informed in part by the physical acoustic signal, but just as important are cultural and contextual clues: where we are, who else is around us, why sounds are being made, and so forth. In a situation where the sound source would be surprising, contextual clues might instead convince us of an inaccurate, but more likely-seeming, source. And without any evidence to contradict that sound-source identification, it persists—“we need no other” (Fales, 58).

Such a contradiction could come through visual evidence. One could look at photos and videos of the musicians performing—for example, “here’s Jimmy Smith playing a Hammond B-3 organ.” But digital music technologies, especially MIDI, mean that even these visual clues are not definitive evidence.

If you saw a 1980s performance, heard a stereotypical “bright” and “clean” digitally synthesized sound, and saw the musician play a DX7, why would you _not_ assume that the sound is generated by the DX7?

But! the DX7 was one of the first instruments to implement MIDI technology, which allows the DX7 to control other instruments, modules, and computers. As a result, even in the 1980s, it was not uncommon to use a DX7 as a MIDI controller that triggered sounds from other rack-mounted synthesizers. What I'm saying is: a whole category of evidence is now unreliable thanks to MIDI.

Another resource for learning about sound sources is musician interviews. This has often been my strategy when researching uses of the DX7: I comb old issues of music trade magazines such as _Keyboard_ or _Electronics & Music Maker_ and read interviews until I find the obligatory question about gear. That's fine for '80s music, but what if you want to know about more recent music?

Music trade magazines barely exist anymore. These things flourished in the 1980s but have died out one by one.

So if a researcher is interested in the instruments of newer popular music, what are they to do? How do they orient themselves in the basic facts of the matter, like “what instrument is this”? The immense timbral flexibility of synthesizers, the black-boxing effect of MIDI, DAWs obviating hardware synthesizers, and the decline of music technology trade magazines seem to create a perfect storm of sound-source illegibility.

As a concrete example, let me recount my search for evidence that a DX7 produced the opening sound in “Versace on the Floor.”

- I checked the liner notes, but specific equipment used for the song is not mentioned.
- I consulted many interviews with Mars and associated musicians, but found no equipment mentioned in them.
- I watched concert footage available on YouTube, but the keyboards were visually obscure since the videos’ focus was Mars rather than his touring keyboardist (John Fossit).
- I was unable to find a way to get in touch with John Fossit.
- I did have a connection to Greg Phillinganes, who is credited for “keyboards” in the liner notes on the album. I thought this would clinch my success, but even he didn’t know what keyboard had been used for the opening—he wasn’t involved in the recording of the song’s opening, and overdubbed the synth lead solo that occurs later in the track.

The takeaway here is that it’s extremely difficult to verify how a song was recorded these days, and while my journey with “Versace” is just one example, I’m certain other similar questions would have similar outcomes.

We’ve reached a point in music technology where the study of instruments must take on a new, perhaps less obvious, form. The advent of MIDI, digital sampling, and the virtualization of music-making renders a study of materiality nearly obsolete. But The Imagined DX7 illustrates that analysis of instruments doesn’t need to rely on concrete factual identification of the instrument in order to lead to meaningful insights.

Indeed, for recent pop music, the question “how was this sound made?” is probably inevitably answered “with a DAW and VST.” Today’s musicians, especially those with the resources of Bruno Mars, can create any sound imaginable.

More interesting than “how was it made?” is “why was it made?”

What does it mean to be a hugely successful Top 40 artist and choose a sound for your record that’s been widely panned as dated, overused, and grating? What’s under examination in that question is the sound itself, the timbre, rather than the object that produced it. This isn’t to say that anything goes, or to encourage intellectual sloppiness or laziness in sound-source identification, but rather to suggest that if the identification works and is productive, perhaps we need no other. Accurately identifying the gear used in the recording is one avenue of research, but how many listeners can discern the difference between Dexed and a DX7? How many care? Does it impact the most interesting parts of my research? Ultimately, what I am interested in is what it means to use a DX7 E. PIANO 1 sound—how listeners in 2016 experience such a distinctly ’80s sound, how the sound might prompt every review I found to mention the track’s retro sound, how the sound connects Bruno Mars to a specific lineage of R&B balladeers. When “Versace” starts, the timbre of the opening synthesizer immediately calls up all these associations of that familiar, even cheesy sound that “must die,” but in a sleek, revitalized, 2016 package. Not just anyone could pull this off, but Mars has the panache and the musical background to make the DX7 sound cool—or if not cool, then at least charming.

It isn’t particularly revolutionary to urge analysts to attend to what the sound means culturally; sound studies has done this for decades. The novelty is in thinking of this as a methodology for the analysis of musical instruments, an area that traditionally (and still) is preoccupied with gear—physical instruments and their material presentations—as sound sources. The critical idea here is that the timbre is the instrument. Timbre can carry the essence of an instrument even when the timbre is produced by something else, as in the case of the Imagined DX7. Timbral similarities, especially when combined with cultural plausibility, encourage the misattribution of a timbre to a sound source. Misattributed or not, the various instances of the (Imagined) DX7 sound all add new nodes to the vast web of intertextual meanings that the DX7 calls up. The practical obsolescence of hardware synthesizers means that traditional organological methods may fail, but even if we can’t know for certain what instrument is used in a recording, we can always learn what listeners believe the instrument to be. Whether they're right or not is immaterial.
